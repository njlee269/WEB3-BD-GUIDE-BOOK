# Regulation And Market Structure

## Why BD needs regulatory fluency

In web3, regulation is not a side topic.

It affects:

- whether a deal can legally exist
- who inside the customer can approve it
- which banking partner will support it
- how long procurement takes
- whether revenue can scale across jurisdictions

BD without regulatory fluency creates pipeline that legal, compliance, or operations later kills.

## Your minimum operating model

For every target market, track:

- asset classification
- licensing or registration perimeter
- AML and travel rule obligations
- stablecoin treatment
- custody expectations
- securities law exposure
- tax and reporting friction

## United States

The U.S. remains strategically important because global companies still benchmark against U.S. policy and capital markets.

What matters now:

- stablecoin policy moved forward materially in 2025
- institutional digital asset products gained more legitimacy
- federal versus state perimeter still matters

Practical BD takeaway:

- even if you do not sell in the U.S. first, you should understand U.S. policy because counterparties, investors, and partners will ask about it

## European Union

MiCA matters because it gives a clearer operating framework than many markets.

Why this matters for BD:

- a clearer framework reduces uncertainty in partner conversations
- licensed entities become better distribution partners
- compliance maturity becomes a selling point, not a drag

Practical BD takeaway:

- if your company can work with MiCA-aligned institutions or practices, you gain credibility elsewhere too

## Hong Kong and Singapore

These markets matter because they are Asian reference points for institutional digital asset business.

What they signal:

- digital asset regulation in Asia is becoming more product-specific and infrastructure-aware
- stablecoin, custody, and market conduct rules are central

Practical BD takeaway:

- if you are Korea-based, follow Hong Kong and Singapore closely because regional buyers compare market readiness across these hubs

## Korea

Korea deserves its own strategic lens.

What matters:

- strong retail awareness already exists
- banking relationships are central to what can scale
- legal clarity is improving, but product design still needs care
- institutional and corporate participation matters more than before

Key business implication:

- in Korea, distribution, trust, and bank-compatible structure can matter more than pure product speed

## Market structure knowledge every BD should build

You should understand the role of:

- issuers
- custodians
- banks
- broker-dealers and securities firms
- exchanges and OTC desks
- market makers
- wallet infrastructure providers
- compliance vendors
- payment service providers

If you cannot map those players, you will not see where your product actually fits.

## The five regulatory questions to ask before serious BD

1. Is the asset or workflow clearly legal in the target market?
2. Does the customer need a license or regulated partner to use the product?
3. Which entity is the merchant of record, custodian, issuer, or settlement principal?
4. What AML, KYC, travel rule, or reporting requirements attach?
5. Which part of the design breaks if the bank says no?

## How regulation changes messaging

Weak BD messaging:

- faster
- decentralized
- onchain
- innovative

Better BD messaging:

- lower settlement friction
- clearer controls
- auditable workflow
- lower operational risk
- better working capital efficiency
- compliance-ready expansion

## Working rule

Never pitch a regulated buyer with a crypto-native vocabulary first.

Start with business problem, controls, and economics.

Only then explain the blockchain component.
